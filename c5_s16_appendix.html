the calculation of \( E\{U_0 | H_0\} \) under the hypothesis \( H_0 \).

Step-by-Step Explanation#

  1. Definition of \( U_0 \):

    Under hypothesis \( H_0 \), the received signal \( y(t) \) is given by: $\( y(t) = u_0(t) + z(t) \)\( where \) u_0(t) \( is the deterministic signal component, and \) z(t) $ is a zero-mean complex Gaussian noise process.

    The decision variable \( U_0 \) is defined as: $\( U_0 = \Re \left\{ \int_0^T y(t) u_0^*(t) \, dt \right\} - \mathcal{E}_0 \)\( where \) \mathcal{E}_0 = \int_0^T |u^{BP}_0(t)|^2 , dt \( is the energy of **bandpass** \) u^{BP}_0(t) \( over the interval \) [0, T] $.

  2. Express \( E\{U_0 | H_0\} \):

    Substitute \( y(t) = u_0(t) + z(t) \) into the expression for \( U_0 \): $\( E\{U_0 | H_0\} = E \left\{ \Re \left[ \int_0^T (u_0(t) + z(t)) u_0^*(t) \, dt \right] \right\} - \mathcal{E}_0 \)$

  3. Separate the Terms Inside the Integral:

    Expand the integral: $\( E\{U_0 | H_0\} = E \left\{ \Re \left[ \int_0^T \left( u_0(t) u_0^*(t) + z(t) u_0^*(t) \right) \, dt \right] \right\} - \mathcal{E}_0 \)$

  4. Break Down the Real Part:

    The real part of a complex integral can be written as the average of the integral and its complex conjugate: $\( E\{U_0 | H_0\} = E \left\{ \frac{1}{2} \int_0^T \left[ u_0(t) u_0^*(t) + z(t) u_0^*(t) \right] \, dt + \frac{1}{2} \int_0^T \left[ u_0^*(t) u_0(t) + z^*(t) u_0(t) \right] \, dt \right\} - \mathcal{E}_0 \)$

    Here, we’ve used the property \( \Re\{x\} = \frac{1}{2}(x + x^*) \) for a complex quantity \( x \).

  5. Separate Terms Based on Expectations:

    Since \( u_0(t) \) is deterministic, and \( z(t) \) is zero-mean with \( E\{z(t)\} = 0 \), the expectation of terms involving \( z(t) \) will vanish: $\( E\{U_0 | H_0\} = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^T u_0(t) u_0^*(t) \, dt + \frac{1}{2} \int_0^T u_0^*(t) u_0(t) \, dt - \mathcal{E}_0 \)$

  6. Simplify Using the Definition of Signal Energy:

    Notice that \( u_0(t) u_0^*(t) = |u_0(t)|^2 \) and \( u_0^*(t) u_0(t) = |u_0(t)|^2 \). Therefore: $\( \int_0^T u_0(t) u_0^*(t) \, dt = \int_0^T |u_0(t)|^2 \, dt = 2\mathcal{E}_0 \)\( and similarly, \)\( \int_0^T u_0^*(t) u_0(t) \, dt = 2\mathcal{E}_0 \)$

    Substitute these into the expression: $\( E\{U_0 | H_0\} = \frac{1}{2} 2\mathcal{E}_0 + \frac{1}{2} 2\mathcal{E}_0 - \mathcal{E}_0 = \mathcal{E}_0 \)$

Final Result#

Thus, the mean of \( U_0 \), given \( H_0 \), is: $\( E\{U_0 | H_0\} = \mathcal{E}_0 \)$

the conditional variance \( \text{Var}(U_0 | H_0) \) under hypothesis \( H_0 \)#

Define: $\( I = \int_0^T z(t) u_0^*(t) \, dt \)\( so that: \)\( U_0 = \Re\{I\} \)$

  1. Variance of \( U_0 \):

    The conditional variance \( \text{Var}(U_0 | H_0) \) is then given by: $\( \text{Var}(U_0 | H_0) = E\left\{ \left( \Re\{I\} \right)^2 \right\} \)\( Using \) \Re{I} = \frac{1}{2}(I + I^*) \(, we have: \)\( \text{Var}(U_0 | H_0) = E\left\{ \left( \frac{1}{2} (I + I^*) \right)^2 \right\} = \frac{1}{4} E\{(I + I^*)^2\} \)$

  2. Expanding \( (I + I^*)^2 \):

    Expand \( (I + I^*)^2 \) to separate the terms: $\( (I + I^*)^2 = I^2 + 2 I I^* + (I^*)^2 \)\( Since \) I \( is an integral involving Gaussian noise \) z(t) \(, it follows that \) I^2 = 0 \( and \) (I^*)^2 = 0 \( due to uncorrelated noise at different times. Thus: \)\( E\{(I + I^*)^2\} = E\{2 I I^*\} = 2 E\{|I|^2\} \)$

    Therefore: $\( \text{Var}(U_0 | H_0) = \frac{1}{4} \cdot 2 E\{|I|^2\} = \frac{1}{2} E\{|I|^2\} \)$

  3. Compute \( E\{|I|^2\} \):

    Now, let’s find \( E\{|I|^2\} \), where \( I = \int_0^T z(t) u_0^*(t) \, dt \): $\( E\{|I|^2\} = E\left\{ \left( \int_0^T z(t_1) u_0^*(t_1) \, dt_1 \right) \left( \int_0^T z^*(t_3) u_0(t_3) \, dt_3 \right) \right\} \)\( Expanding the expectation: \)\( E\{|I|^2\} = \int_0^T \int_0^T E\{z(t_1) z^*(t_3)\} u_0^*(t_1) u_0(t_3) \, dt_1 \, dt_3 \)$

  4. Apply Noise Properties:

    The noise \( z(t) \) is a zero-mean white Gaussian process with autocorrelation: $\( E\{z(t_i) z(t_j)\} = 0, \quad E\{z^*(t_i) z^*(t_j)\} = 0 \)\( \)\( E\{z(t_i) z^*(t_j)\} = 2 N_0 \delta(t_i - t_j) \)$

    Using these properties, only terms of the form \( E\{z(t_1) z^*(t_3)\} \) contribute to the expectation. Thus: $\( E\{|I|^2\} = \int_0^T \int_0^T 2 N_0 \delta(t_1 - t_3) u_0^*(t_1) u_0(t_3) \, dt_1 \, dt_3 \)\( Using the delta function property \) \int f(t) \delta(t - t_0) , dt = f(t_0) \(, we get: \)\( E\{|I|^2\} = 2 N_0 \int_0^T |u_0(t_1)|^2 \, dt_1 \)$

  5. Final Expression for \( \text{Var}(U_0 | H_0) \):

    Substitute \( E\{|I|^2\} \) into the expression for \( \text{Var}(U_0 | H_0) \): $\( \text{Var}(U_0 | H_0) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2 N_0 \int_0^T |u_0(t_1)|^2 \, dt_1 = N_0 \int_0^T |u_0(t_1)|^2 \, dt_1 \)$

Since \( \int_0^T |u_0(t_1)|^2 \, dt_1 = \mathcal{E}_0 \), we can also write: $\( \text{Var}(U_0 | H_0) = N_0 2\mathcal{E}_0 \)$

Summary#

The conditional variance \( \text{Var}(U_0 | H_0) \) is: $\( \text{Var}(U_0 | H_0) = 2N_0 \mathcal{E}_0 \)$

Definition of \( \rho \): Complex-Valued Cross-Correlation Coefficient#

The complex cross-correlation coefficient \( \rho \) between the signals \( u_0(t) \) and \( u_1(t) \) is defined as:

\[ \rho = \rho_r + j \rho_i = \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{\mathcal{E}_0 \mathcal{E}_1}} \int_0^T u_0(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \]

where:

  • \( \rho \) is a complex quantity with real part \( \rho_r \) and imaginary part \( \rho_i \).

Interpretation of \( \rho \):#

  1. Cross-Correlation: The integral \( \int_0^T u_0(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \) measures the similarity between \( u_0(t) \) and \( u_1(t) \) over the interval \( [0, T] \). When \( u_0(t) \) and \( u_1(t) \) are strongly correlated, this integral will have a larger magnitude.

  2. Normalization: Dividing by \( 2 \sqrt{\mathcal{E}_0 \mathcal{E}_1} \) normalizes this quantity, giving \( \rho \) a scale-invariant measure of similarity. This normalization ensures that \( |\rho| \leq 1 \), similar to the standard correlation coefficient.

  3. Real and Imaginary Parts: \( \rho \) is complex because the signals \( u_0(t) \) and \( u_1(t) \) may have phase differences. The real part \( \rho_r \) and imaginary part \( \rho_i \) capture different aspects of the correlation.

Using \( \rho \) to Calculate the Conditional Mean of \( U_1 \) Given \( H_0 \)#

Now, let’s calculate the conditional mean \( E\{U_1 | H_0\} \) given that hypothesis \( H_0 \) is true.

Under \( H_0 \), the received signal \( y(t) \) is modeled as: $\( y(t) = u_0(t) + z(t) \)$

The decision variable \( U_1 \) is defined as: $\( U_1 = \Re \left\{ \int_0^T y(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \right\} - \mathcal{E}_1 \)$

Substitute \( y(t) = u_0(t) + z(t) \) into the expression for \( U_1 \): $\( E\{U_1 | H_0\} = E \left\{ \Re \left[ \int_0^T \left( u_0(t) + z(t) \right) u_1^*(t) \, dt \right] - \mathcal{E}_1 \right\} \)$

Expanding the Integral#

Expand the integral term in \( E\{U_1 | H_0\} \): $\( E\{U_1 | H_0\} = E \left\{ \Re \left[ \int_0^T u_0(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt + \int_0^T z(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \right] - \mathcal{E}_1 \right\} \)$

Separating Terms:#

Now we can separate the expectation into two parts, focusing on each term individually:

  1. First Term \( \int_0^T u_0(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \): This term does not involve noise and is deterministic. Using the definition of \( \rho \), we can express it in terms of \( \rho \): $\( \int_0^T u_0(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt = 2 \sqrt{\mathcal{E}_0 \mathcal{E}_1} \rho \)\( Thus: \)\( \Re \left\{ \int_0^T u_0(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \right\} = 2 \sqrt{\mathcal{E}_0 \mathcal{E}_1} \rho_r \)$

  2. Second Term \( \int_0^T z(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \): This term involves the noise \( z(t) \). Since \( z(t) \) is zero-mean, the expectation of this term is zero: $\( E \left\{ \Re \left( \int_0^T z(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \right) \right\} = 0 \)$

Combining Results:#

Now we can combine the results of these two terms: $\( E\{U_1 | H_0\} = \Re \left\{ 2 \sqrt{\mathcal{E}_0 \mathcal{E}_1} \rho_r \right\} - \mathcal{E}_1 \)$

Thus, the conditional mean of \( U_1 \), given \( H_0 \), is:

\[ E\{U_1 | H_0\} = 2 \sqrt{\mathcal{E}_0 \mathcal{E}_1} \rho_r - \mathcal{E}_1 \]

Summary#

  • The complex-valued cross-correlation coefficient \( \rho = \rho_r + j \rho_i \) quantifies the correlation between \( u_0(t) \) and \( u_1(t) \) in a normalized manner.

  • Using \( \rho \), we express the conditional mean of \( U_1 \) under \( H_0 \) as: $\( E\{U_1 | H_0\} = 2 \sqrt{\mathcal{E}_0 \mathcal{E}_1} \rho_r - \mathcal{E}_1 \)$

  • This result shows that the mean of \( U_1 \) depends linearly on the real part of the cross-correlation between \( u_0(t) \) and \( u_1(t) \), represented by \( \rho_r \), and adjusted by the energy term \( \mathcal{E}_1 \).

the conditional variance \( \text{Var}\{U_1 | H_0\} \)#

Definition of \( U_1 \)#

Given that \( H_0 \) is true, the received signal \( y(t) \) is: $\( y(t) = u_0(t) + z(t) \)\( where \) u_0(t) \( is a deterministic signal and \) z(t) \( is a zero-mean complex Gaussian noise process with power spectral density \) N_0 $.

The decision variable \( U_1 \) is defined as: $\( U_1 = \Re \left\{ \int_0^T y(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \right\} - \mathcal{E}_1 \)\( where: \)\( \mathcal{E}_1 = \int_0^T |u_1(t)|^2 \, dt \)$

Substitute \( y(t) = u_0(t) + z(t) \): $\( U_1 = \Re \left\{ \int_0^T \left( u_0(t) + z(t) \right) u_1^*(t) \, dt \right\} - \mathcal{E}_1 \)\( Expanding this, we have: \)\( U_1 = \Re \left\{ \int_0^T u_0(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt + \int_0^T z(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \right\} - \mathcal{E}_1 \)$

Since \( \int_0^T u_0(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \) is a deterministic quantity, the variance of \( U_1 \) is entirely due to the noise term: $\( U_1 = \Re \left\{ \int_0^T z(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \right\} \)$

Define: $\( I_1 = \int_0^T z(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \)\( Then: \)\( U_1 = \Re\{I_1\} \)$

Variance of \( U_1 \)#

The conditional variance \( \text{Var}\{U_1 | H_0\} \) is therefore: $\( \text{Var}\{U_1 | H_0\} = \text{Var}\{\Re\{I_1\}\} \)\( Since \) \Re{I_1} = \frac{1}{2}(I_1 + I_1^*) \(, we have: \)\( \text{Var}\{\Re\{I_1\}\} = \frac{1}{4} \text{Var}\{I_1 + I_1^*\} = \frac{1}{4} E\{|I_1|^2\} \)$

Compute \( E\{|I_1|^2\} \)#

To compute \( E\{|I_1|^2\} \), where \( I_1 = \int_0^T z(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \), we proceed as follows:

\[ E\{|I_1|^2\} = E\left\{ \left( \int_0^T z(t_1) u_1^*(t_1) \, dt_1 \right) \left( \int_0^T z^*(t_2) u_1(t_2) \, dt_2 \right) \right\} \]

Expanding this expectation: $\( E\{|I_1|^2\} = \int_0^T \int_0^T E\{z(t_1) z^*(t_2)\} u_1^*(t_1) u_1(t_2) \, dt_1 \, dt_2 \)$

Since \( z(t) \) is a complex Gaussian noise process with autocorrelation: $\( E\{z(t_1) z^*(t_2)\} = 2 N_0 \delta(t_1 - t_2) \)\( we substitute this into the integral: \)\( E\{|I_1|^2\} = \int_0^T \int_0^T 2 N_0 \delta(t_1 - t_2) u_1^*(t_1) u_1(t_2) \, dt_1 \, dt_2 \)$

Using the sifting property of the delta function, \( \int f(t) \delta(t - t_0) \, dt = f(t_0) \), we get: $\( E\{|I_1|^2\} = 2 N_0 \int_0^T |u_1(t_1)|^2 \, dt_1 \)$

Since \( \int_0^T |u_1(t_1)|^2 \, dt_1 = 2\mathcal{E}_1 \), we have: $\( E\{|I_1|^2\} = 2 N_0 2\mathcal{E}_1 \)$

Final Expression for \( \text{Var}(U_1 | H_0) \)#

Now we can substitute \( E\{|I_1|^2\} \) back into the expression for \( \text{Var}(U_1 | H_0) \): $\( \text{Var}\{U_1 | H_0\} = \frac{1}{4} \times 2 N_0 2\mathcal{E}_1 = N_0 2\mathcal{E}_1 \)$

Summary#

The conditional variance of \( U_1 \) given \( H_0 \) is: $\( \text{Var}\{U_1 | H_0\} = 2 N_0 \mathcal{E}_1 \)$

This result shows that the variance of \( U_1 \) under \( H_0 \) depends on the noise power spectral density \( N_0 \) and the energy \( \mathcal{E}_1 \) of the signal \( u_1(t) \).

the conditional covariance \( \text{Cov}(U_0, U_1 | H_0) \),#

\( \text{Cov}(U_0, U_1 | H_0) \), which is defined as:

\[ \text{Cov}(U_0, U_1 | H_0) = E\{ U_0 U_1 | H_0 \} - E\{U_0 | H_0\} E\{U_1 | H_0\} \]

Since we already know that \( E\{U_0 | H_0\} = 0 \) and \( E\{U_1 | H_0\} = 2 \sqrt{\mathcal{E}_0 \mathcal{E}_1} \rho_r - \mathcal{E}_1 \), the covariance simplifies to: $\( \text{Cov}(U_0, U_1 | H_0) = E\{ U_0 U_1 | H_0 \} \)$

This is because \( E\{U_0 | H_0\} = 0 \) causes the second term to vanish.

Expression for \( U_0 \) and \( U_1 \)#

Given \( H_0 \), the received signal \( y(t) \) is: $\( y(t) = u_0(t) + z(t) \)\( where \) u_0(t) \( is the deterministic signal and \) z(t) $ is zero-mean complex Gaussian noise.

The decision variables \( U_0 \) and \( U_1 \) are defined as: $\( U_0 = \Re \left\{ \int_0^T y(t) u_0^*(t) \, dt \right\} - \mathcal{E}_0 \)\( \)\( U_1 = \Re \left\{ \int_0^T y(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \right\} - \mathcal{E}_1 \)$

Since \( U_0 \) and \( U_1 \) only involve noise contributions in their non-deterministic parts, we can express them as: $\( U_0 = \Re \left\{ \int_0^T z(t) u_0^*(t) \, dt \right\} \)\( \)\( U_1 = \Re \left\{ \int_0^T z(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \right\} \)$

Let: $\( I_0 = \int_0^T z(t) u_0^*(t) \, dt \)\( \)\( I_1 = \int_0^T z(t) u_1^*(t) \, dt \)$

Then we have: $\( U_0 = \Re\{I_0\}, \quad U_1 = \Re\{I_1\} \)$

Covariance \( E\{ U_0 U_1 | H_0 \} \)#

To calculate \( E\{ U_0 U_1 | H_0 \} \), we expand \( U_0 U_1 \) using the real parts of \( I_0 \) and \( I_1 \): $\( U_0 U_1 = \Re\{I_0\} \cdot \Re\{I_1\} = \frac{1}{4} (I_0 + I_0^*)(I_1 + I_1^*) \)$

Expanding this product: $\( U_0 U_1 = \frac{1}{4} \left( I_0 I_1 + I_0 I_1^* + I_0^* I_1 + I_0^* I_1^* \right) \)$

Since \( I_0 \) and \( I_1 \) are integrals involving the noise \( z(t) \), terms like \( I_0 I_1 \) and \( I_0^* I_1^* \) will vanish in expectation due to the independence of \( z(t) \) and \( z^*(t) \) at different times. Thus: $\( E\{U_0 U_1 | H_0\} = \frac{1}{4} \left( E\{ I_0 I_1^* \} + E\{ I_0^* I_1 \} \right) \)$

Calculating \( E\{ I_0 I_1^* \} \)#

Now, we calculate \( E\{ I_0 I_1^* \} \), where: $\( I_0 = \int_0^T z(t_1) u_0^*(t_1) \, dt_1 \)\( \)\( I_1^* = \int_0^T z^*(t_3) u_1(t_3) \, dt_3 \)$

Thus: $\( E\{ I_0 I_1^* \} = E \left\{ \int_0^T \int_0^T z(t_1) z^*(t_3) u_0^*(t_1) u_1(t_3) \, dt_1 \, dt_3 \right\} \)$

Using the autocorrelation property of the noise \( z(t) \), we have: $\( E\{z(t_1) z^*(t_3)\} = 2 N_0 \delta(t_1 - t_3) \)$

Substitute this into the expectation: $\( E\{ I_0 I_1^* \} = \int_0^T \int_0^T 2 N_0 \delta(t_1 - t_3) u_0^*(t_1) u_1(t_3) \, dt_1 \, dt_3 \)$

The delta function \( \delta(t_1 - t_3) \) simplifies the double integral to a single integral: $\( E\{ I_0 I_1^* \} = 2 N_0 \int_0^T u_0^*(t_1) u_1(t_1) \, dt_1 \)$

Similarly, we can compute \( E\{ I_0^* I_1 \} \): $\( E\{ I_0^* I_1 \} = 2 N_0 \int_0^T u_0(t_2) u_1^*(t_2) \, dt_2 \)$

Putting It All Together#
